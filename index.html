CRRT Knowledge Prep Guide

Before your Phase 2 consultant interviews
What this is: The concepts, numbers, trial names, and vocabulary you need so that when a consultant explains their reasoning, you can follow it, recognise when they say something important, and ask the right follow-up. You don't need to memorise everything — focus on understanding the why behind each concept.

Foundations — how CRRT actually works

🔬 The CRRT machine in 60 seconds

Blood is pulled from the patient through a catheter (usually internal jugular or femoral vein), pumped through a filter (haemofilter/dialyser), and returned. While in the filter, waste products and excess fluid are removed. The machine runs continuously (24 hours/day) — unlike regular dialysis which is done in 3–4 hour sessions. This gentleness is why CRRT is used for haemodynamically unstable ICU patients.

⚡ The two clearance mechanisms

Every CRRT decision traces back to these two physics principles. Understand these and you understand modality selection.

DIFFUSION
Concentration gradient
⬇
Solutes move from high concentration (blood) to low concentration (dialysate) across the membrane
Best for: small molecules
(K⁺, Na⁺, urea, creatinine)
CONVECTION
Pressure gradient
⬇
Water is pushed through the membrane by pressure, dragging solutes with it ("solvent drag")
Best for: medium molecules
(cytokines, myoglobin ~17 kDa)

Third mechanism — ADSORPTION: Some molecules stick to the filter membrane itself. This is how Oxiris removes endotoxins. Not a modality choice — it's a filter choice.

☕ The four modalities

Think of these as different coffee brewing methods — each uses a different combination of the mechanisms above.

CVVH
Convection only
Replacement fluid in, ultrafiltrate out. No dialysate. Like a French press.
CVVHD
Diffusion only
Dialysate flows past the membrane. No replacement fluid. Like pour-over.
CVVHDF
Both
Dialysate AND replacement fluid. Most versatile. The default.
SCUF
UF only
Pure fluid removal. No clearance. For volume overload alone.

💧 Key fluids you'll hear about

  • Regiocit — Citrate solution infused pre-filter. The citrate binds calcium in the blood, preventing clotting in the circuit. The machine controls the dose. Only used with citrate anticoagulation.
  • Biphozyl — Bicarbonate-based replacement/dialysate fluid used with citrate protocols. Contains phosphate (important because CRRT strips phosphate).
  • Prismasol — Standard bicarbonate-based replacement/dialysate fluid used with heparin or no-anticoagulation protocols.
  • Calcium gluconate — Infused back into the patient after the filter to replace the calcium that citrate chelated. Without this, the patient becomes hypocalcaemic.

📐 Dose — what the number means

CRRT dose = total effluent volume per kilogram per hour (mL/kg/h). Effluent = all the fluid that leaves the circuit (dialysate out + ultrafiltrate + any replacement fluid that passes through the filter).

Prescribed vs delivered: You prescribe 30 mL/kg/h but the machine doesn't run 100% of the time — filter changes, alarms, transport to CT, etc. Real-world delivery is about 72% of prescribed. So you prescribe higher to deliver 20–25 mL/kg/h, which is the evidence-based target.

Why not just go higher? Two massive trials (RENAL and ATN, >2,600 patients combined) showed absolutely no benefit from higher doses. More dose = more electrolyte swings, more drug clearance, more circuit problems, same mortality.

🩸 The CRRT circuit — what goes where

Follow the blood path:

  1. Vascular catheter — blood is drawn from the patient
  2. Pre-blood pump (pre-BP) — where Regiocit (citrate) is infused if using citrate anticoagulation
  3. Blood pump — drives flow at 100–250 mL/min (varies by protocol)
  4. Pre-filter — where pre-dilution replacement fluid is added (dilutes blood → protects filter from clotting but reduces clearance efficiency)
  5. Filter (haemofilter) — the actual membrane where clearance happens. M100 or Oxiris in your unit
  6. Post-filter — where post-dilution replacement fluid is added (doesn't dilute blood entering filter → better clearance but higher clotting risk)
  7. Return to patient — where calcium is reinfused (for citrate protocols)

Pre vs post dilution: Pre-dilution = safer filter, less efficient clearance. Post-dilution = more efficient, higher clotting risk. Citrate protocols are mostly post-filter replacement (Biphozyl). No-anticoag protocols use high pre-filter fraction (70/30) to protect the circuit.

Q1 — RRT initiation & timing

Evidence: Q2.1 complete (HIGH)

🔬 The core concept: the "sweet spot"

The biggest question in CRRT initiation: start early or wait? The evidence says neither extreme is best. There's a sweet spot:

  • Too early (STARRT-AKI accelerated arm) — no survival benefit, more harm, more patients on dialysis unnecessarily, higher long-term RRT dependence
  • Just right (AKIKI standard/delayed arm) — watchful waiting is safe; 35–40% of patients never need RRT at all
  • Too late (AKIKI-2 more-delayed arm) — oliguria >72 hours or BUN >112 → mortality increases (HR 1.65)

The exception: life-threatening indications always trigger immediate start. No debate, no waiting. K⁺ ≥6.5, pH <7.15, refractory pulmonary oedema, symptomatic uraemia (encephalopathy, pericarditis).

🧠 What the consultant is actually deciding

When there's no single absolute indication, the intensivist is weighing multiple marginal criteria simultaneously. This is the hardest reasoning to extract — it's often gestalt. Your probes should try to decompose it:

  • Is the weighting additive (3 moderate things = start) or hierarchical (one factor dominates)?
  • What's their reassessment cadence? Every 6 hours? Every lab draw? Fixed time?
  • Do they adjust for diagnosis? (Septic AKI might get more patience than rhabdomyolysis)
  • How do they identify the patient who will recover without dialysis? (Urine output trend? Creatinine trajectory? Furosemide stress test?)

📚 Trials they'll reference

STARRT-AKI (NEJM 2020, n=3019)
Accelerated (within 12h of KDIGO 2–3) vs standard (wait for conventional trigger). Multinational RCT.
→ No benefit from early start. Accelerated arm had MORE RRT dependence at 90 days (10.4% vs 6.0%).
AKIKI (JAMA 2016, n=620)
Early (within 6h of KDIGO 3) vs delayed (wait for absolute indication). French multicenter.
→ 49% of delayed arm NEVER received RRT. No mortality difference.
AKIKI-2 (Lancet 2021, n=278)
Extended delayed strategy, pushing even further than AKIKI.
→ HARMFUL. HR 1.65 for 60-day mortality. Oliguria >72h or BUN >112 = too late.
IDEAL-ICU (NEJM 2018, n=488)
Septic shock + RIFLE-F. Early (within 12h) vs delayed (wait 48h).
→ Stopped for futility. 38% of delayed arm avoided RRT entirely.
ELAIN (JAMA 2016, n=231)
Single-centre, surgical/cardiac ICU, biomarker-enriched (NGAL >150).
→ The OUTLIER. Showed benefit from early start — but single-centre, surgical population, not generalizable to medical ICU. Consultants may cite this; know that it's considered an outlier by the field.

👂 Listen for these phrases

👂
"trajectory" or "trend" — They're describing how they predict the future. Ask: "What trend specifically tips you over into starting?"
👂
"additive" or "composite" — They're describing how they combine multiple criteria. This is gold for REINA — probe deeper.
👂
"furosemide stress test" — A bolus of furosemide to see if the kidneys respond. If urine output increases, the kidneys may recover. If not, RRT is more likely needed.
👂
"NGAL" or "KIM-1" — Biomarkers of kidney injury. Not widely used clinically but some centres use them to predict recovery.

⚠️ Don't confuse these

  • AKI stage ≠ RRT indication — A patient can be KDIGO stage 3 (severe AKI by lab criteria) but not need RRT if they're making urine and electrolytes are stable
  • Oliguria ≠ anuria — Oliguria (<0.5 mL/kg/h) is concerning but not an absolute indication. Anuria is much more alarming.
  • Rising creatinine ≠ need for dialysis — Creatinine can rise because GFR dropped from 60 to 40 (not great, not emergency). The rate and context matter.

Q2 — Anticoagulation selection

Evidence: Q3.1–3.5 pending

🔬 Why does the circuit need anticoagulation?

Blood outside the body activates the clotting cascade. Platelets activate, fibrin forms, and the filter clots. A clotted filter = circuit failure = treatment interruption = wasted blood + lost dose. Anticoagulation keeps the circuit running longer. The choice is between:

  • Citrate (regional) — anticoagulates only the circuit, not the patient. Citrate chelates calcium (calcium is essential for clotting), then calcium is replaced after the filter. The patient's systemic clotting is unaffected.
  • Heparin (systemic) — anticoagulates the patient AND the circuit. Risk: bleeding everywhere, not just in the circuit.
  • None — no anticoagulation. Shorter circuit life but no anticoag-related risks. Relies on high pre-dilution and blood flow to keep the circuit open.

🧪 Citrate — the details that matter

Citrate is the default because it's regional — no systemic bleeding risk. But it has quirks:

  • Citrate is metabolised by the liver → If liver failure, citrate accumulates → metabolic acidosis + hypocalcaemia → dangerous. This is the main contraindication.
  • Citrate accumulation detection: Total calcium rises (citrate-calcium complexes) while ionised calcium drops. The ratio of total:ionised calcium >2.5 signals accumulation.
  • Blood flow is fixed at 150 mL/min for citrate because the Regiocit dose is flow-dependent. Higher flow = more citrate delivery = more toxicity risk.
  • Citrate adds sodium — important for hypernatraemic patients. Regiocit contributes a sodium load that can worsen hypernatraemia.
  • Citrate is metabolised to bicarbonate — it acts as a buffer. This is actually helpful in acidotic patients but can cause alkalosis if not monitored.

👂 Listen for these phrases

👂
"calcium gap" or "rising total:ionised ratio" — They're describing early citrate accumulation. Ask: "What ratio makes you worried before 2.5?"
👂
"HIT" — Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia. An immune reaction to heparin causing paradoxical clotting. Cannot use heparin. Alternatives: argatroban (direct thrombin inhibitor) or citrate.
👂
"filtration fraction" — The percentage of plasma water removed by the filter. High FF (>25%) means more haemoconcentration → faster clotting. Pre-dilution reduces FF.
👂
"regional vs systemic" — The fundamental distinction. Regional = circuit only. Systemic = whole patient. This is why citrate wins for bleeding patients.

⚠️ Critical correction already in REINA

  • High bleeding risk → citrate is APPROPRIATE (not contraindicated). Citrate is regional — it doesn't make the patient bleed. The original protocol had this wrong.
  • Liver failure → citrate is CONTRAINDICATED (because liver can't metabolise citrate). This is the real red line, not bleeding.
  • ECMO patients — already on systemic heparin for the ECMO circuit. Use heparin for CRRT too (they're already anticoagulated systemically). Adding citrate on top is unnecessary complexity.

Q3 — Filter selection (Oxiris vs M100)

Evidence: Q5.1 pending (RED)

🔬 What's the difference?

Your unit has two filters:

  • M100 — Standard high-flux polysulfone membrane. Does the job for conventional CRRT needs (solute clearance, volume removal). The workhorse.
  • Oxiris — AN69-based membrane with three layers: (1) a heparin-grafted surface (reduces clotting on the membrane), (2) a polyethylenimine (PEI) layer that adsorbs endotoxins, and (3) the standard AN69 membrane. It does everything M100 does plus endotoxin/cytokine adsorption.

Key insight: Oxiris does not prolong circuit life (Protocol v6 explicitly states this). Its added value is endotoxin removal, primarily in Gram-negative sepsis where lipopolysaccharide (LPS/endotoxin) is driving the inflammatory cascade.

🦠 When Oxiris might matter

This is the question REINA currently can't answer — and the reason this interview domain is critical. The evidence base is thin, so you're extracting expert clinical reasoning. The logic probably involves:

  • Source of sepsis — abdominal (likely Gram-negative) vs pneumonia (often Gram-positive). Endotoxin is a Gram-negative phenomenon.
  • Severity markers — vasopressor dose, lactate level, trajectory of deterioration
  • Timing — early in sepsis course (when endotoxin load is highest) vs late
  • Assessment of response — what does "working" look like? Vasopressor reduction? Lactate clearance? By when?

👂 Listen for these phrases

👂
"endotoxin activity assay" (EAA) — A blood test measuring endotoxin levels. Some centres use this to decide on Oxiris. Ask if your unit has access.
👂
"gestalt" or "clinical picture" — They may not have specific triggers. This is valuable information — it means REINA needs a "consider Oxiris" prompt with clinical context rather than a hard rule.
👂
"vasopressor trajectory" — How they assess whether Oxiris is working. A 20% reduction in noradrenaline over 12 hours? Ask for specifics.
👂
"cost" — Oxiris filters are significantly more expensive than M100. Resource stewardship matters.

Q4 — Dose deviation

Evidence: Q1.1 + Q1.2 complete (HIGH)

🔬 The dose question is settled — mostly

This is REINA's strongest evidence domain. Two landmark trials (RENAL + ATN, 2,600+ patients) showed no benefit from higher doses. 20–25 mL/kg/h delivered is the ceiling. The residual questions are about exceptions:

  • Obesity — do you use actual weight (overtreat), adjusted weight, or ideal weight? No consensus.
  • Rhabdomyolysis — massive myoglobin load. Some clinicians dose higher empirically but there's no RCT supporting this.
  • Tumour lysis syndrome (TLS) — rapidly rising K⁺, phosphate, urate. May need aggressive clearance.
  • Drug clearance — CRRT removes water-soluble drugs (many antibiotics). Dose adjustments needed.
  • HVHF in sepsis — definitively does NOT work. IVOIRE (70 vs 35 mL/kg/h) showed no benefit. Cytokine removal is by adsorption, not convection volume.

📚 Trials they'll reference

RENAL (NEJM 2009, n=1508)
40 vs 25 mL/kg/h effluent. 35 Australian/NZ ICUs. Post-dilution CVVHDF.
→ 44.7% vs 44.7% mortality. Literally identical. OR 1.00 (CI 0.81–1.23).
ATN (NEJM 2008, n=1124)
Intensive (IHD 6x/wk or CVVHDF 35) vs less-intensive (IHD 3x/wk or CVVHDF 20). US VA hospitals.
→ 53.6% vs 51.5%. No difference. Higher dose = more electrolyte problems.
IVOIRE (ICM 2013, n=~140)
70 vs 35 mL/kg/h CVVH in septic shock patients.
→ No survival benefit. Stopped early for slow recruitment. The definitive "no" for HVHF in sepsis.

👂 Listen for these phrases

👂
"adjusted body weight" — Formula: IBW + 0.4 × (actual − IBW). Used in obese patients. Ask at what BMI they switch.
👂
"downtime" — Circuit interruptions. Every hour off CRRT = dose not delivered. This is why prescribed ≠ delivered.
👂
"cytokine clearance" or "HVHF" — If they mention pushing dose for sepsis, this is your opportunity to explore how they handle the evidence gap. The evidence says no benefit — does it change their practice?

Q5 — Discontinuation & step-down

Evidence: Q7.1–7.2 pending (RED)

🔬 The biggest REINA gap

This domain is entirely unbuilt in REINA and there's minimal published evidence. The clinical reasoning you capture here will be REINA's only source of truth. Key concepts:

  • Renal recovery — the kidney starts working again. The most reliable signal is spontaneous urine output (kidneys making urine without diuretic support, or responding to furosemide).
  • Trial off — stopping CRRT temporarily to see if the patient copes. If electrolytes stay stable and urine output is adequate, CRRT might not need to restart.
  • Step-down — reducing dose, switching to SCUF, or transitioning to intermittent haemodialysis (IHD) as a bridge to stopping completely.
  • IHD transition — moving from continuous to intermittent (3–4 hours, 3x/week). Requires haemodynamic stability because IHD removes fluid faster.

📊 Numbers you might hear

0.5 mL/kg/h
Common urine output target for trial off
~400–500 mL/day
Absolute UO some use as threshold
6–24 hours
Typical trial-off observation window
5–7 days
Median CRRT duration in ICU

👂 Listen for these phrases

👂
"creatinine clearance" — They may use a timed urine collection to estimate residual kidney function before stopping.
👂
"furosemide challenge" — Bolus of furosemide before or during trial-off. If kidneys respond with urine, more confidence they're recovering.
👂
"rebound hyperkalaemia" — K⁺ rising after CRRT stops. The most common reason to restart.
👂
"chronic dialysis" or "CKD-5" — The prognostic question. At some point, prolonged CRRT without recovery raises the question of whether this is now permanent kidney failure. Consultants may have a timeframe in mind.

Q6 — Modality selection [NEW]

Evidence: Q4.1 pending

🔬 When does the default change?

REINA defaults to CVVHDF (combination). This domain asks: when does a consultant specifically override that? The key reasoning patterns:

  • CVVHD chosen when: isolated small-molecule problem (hyperkalaemia, dysnatraemia), pure diffusion gives more predictable correction, circuit simplicity matters
  • CVVH chosen when: volume removal is the primary goal AND medium-molecule clearance matters (rhabdomyolysis/myoglobin). But caveat: CVVH + citrate has NO dialysate → limited K⁺ clearance
  • SCUF chosen when: isolated volume overload, no clearance needed at all (e.g., cardiorenal patient needing decongestion only)
  • CVVHDF remains default when: multiple goals, uncertainty, or "I want to cover everything"

👂 Listen for these phrases

👂
"it doesn't really matter" — If they say modality choice rarely changes outcomes, that's important data too. It means REINA's default-with-rare-override approach is validated.
👂
"simplicity" or "fewer bags" — CVVHD uses only dialysate (no replacement fluid). Simpler circuit, fewer fluid connections. Some prefer it for this reason alone.

Q7 — Fluid removal & UF rate [NEW]

No evidence query planned

🔬 UF rate — the daily titration question

Ultrafiltration (UF) = fluid removal from the patient. This is separate from clearance dose. The UF rate is how fast you're removing water. Typical range: 1.0–1.75 mL/kg/h (or roughly 50–150 mL/h for an average patient).

The dilemma: fluid-overloaded patients need decongestion (lungs wet, oedematous), but removing fluid too fast drops blood pressure. This tension between decongestion and haemodynamic stability is negotiated multiple times per shift.

💡 Concepts to understand

  • Fluid overload % — (cumulative fluid in − cumulative fluid out) / admission weight × 100. >10% is associated with worse outcomes. Some consultants target a specific % reduction.
  • VExUS — Venous Excess Ultrasound Score. Uses IVC, hepatic vein, portal vein, and renal vein Doppler to assess venous congestion. Increasingly used to guide fluid removal.
  • Refilling rate — When you remove intravascular fluid, the interstitial fluid takes time to refill the vessels. If you remove faster than refilling, blood pressure drops. This is why aggressive UF in poorly perfused patients is dangerous.
  • MAP threshold — Mean Arterial Pressure. Most units have a MAP target (often ≥65 mmHg). Dropping below triggers UF reduction or vasopressor uptitration.

👂 Listen for these phrases

👂
"net negative" — They're describing the goal of removing more fluid than goes in. "I want this patient 100 mL/h net negative." This is the UF rate minus all infusions.
👂
"back off" or "hold the UF" — They're describing when they pause fluid removal. Ask: "What specifically made you stop — a MAP number, a vasopressor increase, or something else?"
👂
"B-lines" — Lung ultrasound artefacts indicating pulmonary oedema. More B-lines = more lung water = keep removing fluid.

Q8 — Monitoring & real-time titration [NEW]

Evidence: Q8.1–8.2 pending

🔬 What's being monitored and why

  • Ionised calcium (iCa²⁺) — for citrate patients. Both post-filter (to check circuit anticoagulation is adequate: target 0.25–0.35) and systemic (to check patient isn't hypocalcaemic: target 1.0–1.2). Checked Q4–6H.
  • Total:ionised calcium ratio — for citrate accumulation detection. Normally <2.5. A rising ratio with dropping ionised calcium = citrate building up = liver can't metabolise it fast enough.
  • Electrolytes (K⁺, Na⁺, Mg²⁺, PO₄³⁻) — CRRT strips these. Checked Q4–6H. Magnesium and phosphate particularly vulnerable.
  • Blood gas (pH, lactate, HCO₃⁻) — acid-base status. Citrate metabolism produces bicarbonate; if liver fails, both acidosis (from accumulated citrate) and alkalosis (from unmeasured bicarbonate) can occur.
  • Circuit pressures — access pressure (too negative = catheter suctioning), filter pressure, return pressure, transmembrane pressure (TMP). Rising pressures signal clotting.

📈 What "pattern recognition" means here

This domain is about trend reading — seeing the problem 2–4 hours before the alarm fires. You're asking consultants to articulate their intuition. Examples:

  • "The iCa²⁺ dropped from 1.15 to 1.05 over two draws — it's not below range yet, but the slope concerns me."
  • "Access pressure went from −80 to −120 over 6 hours — the catheter is starting to malposition or there's thrombus forming."
  • "Potassium keeps swinging despite a stable prescription — there's something else going on (rhabdomyolysis release? cell lysis? insulin timing?)."

👂 Listen for these phrases

👂
"the slope" or "the trend" — They're describing rate of change as more important than absolute values. This is exactly what REINA needs — trend-based alerts, not just threshold-based.
👂
"TMP" — Transmembrane pressure. Rising TMP = membrane is clogging. The earliest clotting signal on the machine.
👂
"proactive change" vs "reactive" — Do they change circuits at 24h prophylactically or wait until it alarms? This reveals their monitoring philosophy.

⚡ The five absolute (emergency) indications

These always trigger immediate RRT start — no debate, no waiting:

  1. Refractory hyperkalaemia — K⁺ ≥6.5 mmol/L unresponsive to medical management
  2. Severe metabolic acidosis — pH <7.15 not correctable by treating the cause
  3. Refractory pulmonary oedema — fluid overload causing respiratory failure, unresponsive to diuretics
  4. Symptomatic uraemia — encephalopathy, pericarditis, uraemic bleeding
  5. Dialysable toxin ingestion — poisoning with a substance CRRT can remove (methanol, ethylene glycol, lithium, salicylates — check EXTRIP guidelines)

🧮 GOLDMARK mnemonic for metabolic acidosis

When a consultant mentions GOLDMARK, they're systematically working through causes of high anion gap metabolic acidosis:

  • Glycols (ethylene glycol, propylene glycol)
  • Oxoproline (paracetamol metabolite)
  • L-lactate (sepsis, shock, hypoperfusion)
  • D-lactate (short bowel syndrome)
  • Methanol
  • Aspirin (salicylates)
  • Renal failure (uraemic acidosis)
  • Ketoacidosis (DKA, starvation, alcoholic)

The key insight for CRRT: lactic acidosis from septic shock needs source treatment (antibiotics, fluids, vasopressors), not CRRT. Ketoacidosis needs insulin, not CRRT. CRRT is only appropriate for acidosis where the kidneys ARE the problem or where a dialysable toxin is the cause.
